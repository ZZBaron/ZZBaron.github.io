Project Overview

This simulation demonstrates a complex market dynamics system with interacting agents, product choices, and cognitive decision-making processes. The code models various agents with different preferences and thresholds in different network structures, simulating product adoption and market share evolution over time.

Key Features

The simulation includes:

  • Multi-agent system with heterogeneous preferences and decision thresholds
  • Multiple network structures (small-world and scale-free networks)
  • Four distinct cognitive modes for decision making (repetition, deliberation, imitation, social comparison)
  • Dynamic market share formation based on individual and social utility
  • Variable product satisfaction capability (alpha parameter)
  • Different levels of social visibility (low, medium, high)
  • Analysis of market dominance using Gini coefficient
  • Measurement of market turbulence (rate of product switching)
  • Visualization of market shares and cognitive mode adoption over time

Mathematical Overview

Individual and Social Utility

Agents calculate expected utility based on individual preferences and social influence:

\[ U_i(j) = \alpha \cdot (\beta_i \cdot I_{ij} + (1-\beta_i) \cdot S_{ij}) \]

where \(U_i(j)\) is agent \(i\)'s utility for product \(j\), \(I_{ij}\) is individual utility based on product-preference match, \(S_{ij}\) is social utility (fraction of friends using product), \(\beta_i\) is the agent's weighting parameter, and \(\alpha\) is the product's ability to satisfy.

Individual Utility Calculation

Individual utility decreases with distance between product dimension and agent preference:

\[ I_{ij} = 1 - |d_j - p_i| \]

where \(d_j\) is product \(j\)'s dimension value and \(p_i\) is agent \(i\)'s preference.

Uncertainty

Agents experience uncertainty based on social adoption:

\[ Unc_i(j) = (1-\beta_i) \cdot (1-S_{ij}) \]

where \(Unc_i(j)\) is agent \(i\)'s uncertainty about product \(j\).

Cognitive Modes

Four decision-making processes determine product choices:

  1. Repetition: When satisfied and certain, agents repeat their previous choice.
  2. Deliberation: When not satisfied but certain, agents choose based on expected utility:

    3. \[ P(j) = \frac{e^{b_1 \cdot U_i(j)}}{\sum_k e^{b_1 \cdot U_i(k)}} \]

  3. Imitation: When satisfied but uncertain, agents imitate friends:

    4. \[ P(j) = \frac{e^{b_2 \cdot S_{ij}}}{\sum_k e^{b_2 \cdot S_{ik}}} \]

  4. Social Comparison: When neither satisfied nor certain, agents consider only products used by friends.

Network Structures

Two network types are implemented:

  1. Small-world networks: Based on Watts-Strogatz model with rewiring probability \(s\).
  2. Scale-free networks: Based on Barabasi-Albert model with node deactivation probability \(d\).

Market Dominance

Market dominance is measured using the Gini coefficient over market shares:

\[ G = \frac{\sum_{i=1}^{M}\sum_{j=1}^{M}|s_i - s_j|}{2M\sum_{i=1}^{M}s_i} \]

where \(s_i\) is the market share of product \(i\) and \(M\) is the number of products.

Simulation Methods

The simulation uses several methods to model complex market behavior:

  1. Agent-based modeling: Individual agents make autonomous decisions based on their preferences, thresholds, and social connections.
  2. Network modeling: Social connections are structured following established network models from complex systems theory.
  3. Cognitive process simulation: Different decision-making processes are triggered based on satisfaction and certainty thresholds.
  4. Probabilistic choice: Product selection uses logit models with varying sensitivity parameters.
  5. Parameter sweeping: Systematic exploration of parameter space to identify emergent patterns.

Applications

This market dynamics simulation can be applied to study various phenomena including:

  • Product adoption and diffusion in social networks
  • Impact of social visibility on market concentration
  • Effects of product satisfaction capability on market stability
  • Network structure influence on market dominance
  • Cognitive decision-making processes in consumer choice
  • Emergence of market monopolies or diversity
  • Conditions leading to high market turbulence
  • Effectiveness of marketing strategies in different network environments
