Black Hole Light Ray Simulation

View on GitHub

Project Overview

This simulation demonstrates the gravitational lensing effect of a black hole on light rays in a two-dimensional space. The visualization shows how light paths bend around a massive object according to Einstein's theory of general relativity, creating visual phenomena such as gravitational lensing and photon spheres.

Key Features

The simulation includes:

  • Visualization of light ray trajectories in Schwarzschild geometry
  • Animation of light propagation around a black hole
  • Accurate simulation of null geodesics using relativistic equations
  • Visualization of the black hole's event horizon
  • Demonstration of gravitational lensing effects
  • Interactive visualization with customizable parameters

Schwarzschild Geometry

The simulation uses the Schwarzschild metric, which describes the geometry of spacetime around a non-rotating, spherically symmetric mass:

\[ ds^2 = -\left(1-\frac{r_s}{r}\right)c^2dt^2 + \left(1-\frac{r_s}{r}\right)^{-1}dr^2 + r^2d\Omega^2 \]

where \(r_s = \frac{2GM}{c^2}\) is the Schwarzschild radius, \(M\) is the mass of the black hole, \(G\) is the gravitational constant, and \(c\) is the speed of light.

Schwarzschild Radius

The Schwarzschild radius defines the event horizon of a black hole:

\[ r_s = \frac{2GM}{c^2} \]

In the simulation, we use normalized units where \(G = c = 1\), so the Schwarzschild radius becomes \(r_s = 2M\).

Null Geodesics

Light rays follow null geodesics, which are paths where the spacetime interval is zero (\(ds^2 = 0\)). The equations of motion for these geodesics can be derived from the Lagrangian:

\[ \mathcal{L} = \frac{1}{2}g_{\mu\nu}\frac{dx^\mu}{d\lambda}\frac{dx^\nu}{d\lambda} \]

where \(\lambda\) is an affine parameter along the geodesic.

Geodesic Equations

For motion in the equatorial plane (\(\theta = \pi/2\)), the geodesic equations reduce to:

\[ \frac{dr}{d\lambda} = f(r) \cdot p_r \]

\[ \frac{d\phi}{d\lambda} = \frac{L}{r^2} \]

\[ \frac{dp_r}{d\lambda} = \frac{L^2}{r^3}f(r) - \frac{r_s}{2r^2}f(r) \]

\[ \frac{dL}{d\lambda} = 0 \]

where \(f(r) = 1 - \frac{r_s}{r}\), \(p_r\) is the radial momentum component, and \(L\) is the angular momentum (conserved).

Effective Potential

The motion of light rays can be understood through an effective potential:

\[ V_{eff}(r) = \frac{L^2}{2r^2}\left(1 - \frac{r_s}{r}\right) \]

The closest approach of a light ray to the black hole is determined by the impact parameter \(b = \frac{L}{E}\), where \(E\) is the energy of the photon.

Critical Radius

For a non-rotating black hole (Schwarzschild black hole), the photon sphere occurs at \(r = \frac{3r_s}{2}\), where light rays can orbit the black hole in unstable circular orbits.

Light rays with impact parameters less than the critical value \(b_{crit} = \frac{3\sqrt{3}r_s}{2}\) will be captured by the black hole.

Simulation Methods

In the code, the following methods are used to simulate light propagation:

  1. Coordinate transformation: Converting between Cartesian and polar coordinates with proper metric components
  2. Numerical integration: Using the RK45 algorithm with adaptive step size to solve the geodesic equations
  3. Animation technique: Progressively revealing light ray paths to visualize their propagation over time
